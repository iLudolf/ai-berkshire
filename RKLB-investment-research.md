## Step 2: Business Fundamentals Analysis — Duan Yongping's "Right Business"

### One-Sentence Definition

**Rocket Lab's business is: a "full-stack provider" of space infrastructure — designing and manufacturing satellite components and satellites for customers, then launching them on its own rockets.**

If a simpler analogy is needed: **Foxconn + SF Express of the space world — build it for you, then ship it up.**

### Business Model Canvas

Rocket Lab is not a "launch company" — launch accounts for only 33% of revenue. Its core is a **space systems integrator**:

1. **Space Systems (67% of revenue)**: Reaction wheels, star trackers, solar cells, separation systems, laser communication terminals, flight software, satellite platforms. Built through 7 acquisitions, covering nearly all critical segments of satellite manufacturing.
2. **Launch Services (33% of revenue)**: Electron small rocket (300 kg LEO capacity), Neutron medium rocket (13-ton LEO, maiden flight targeted Q4 2026).
3. **Potential third leg**: SDA missile defense satellite constellation ($816M contract), upgrading from "selling components" to "full satellite delivery + on-orbit operations."

**Revenue model**: Project-based (satellite manufacturing) + per-launch fees (launch services) + long-term operations contracts. Not subscription-based, but government contracts provide multi-year revenue visibility.

### Gross Margin Analysis

GAAP gross margin improved from -3% in FY2021 to 34.4% in FY2025, further reaching 38.2% in Q1 2026. Non-GAAP gross margin is 43%.

**Peer comparison**:
- Traditional aerospace/defense (Northrop Grumman, L3Harris): gross margin 25-30%
- SpaceX (private, estimated): launch segment gross margin estimated 50%+ (reuse economics)
- Rocket Lab's 34% gross margin sits "better than traditional defense, behind SpaceX"

Drivers of sustained gross margin expansion: (1) higher share of Space Systems revenue, which is more profitable; (2) increased Electron launch cadence spreading fixed costs; (3) vertical integration reducing external procurement costs.

### Operating Leverage Analysis

Revenue grew from $62M in FY2021 to $602M in FY2025 (~10x), while net loss expanded from -$117M to only -$198M (~1.7x). Operating margin improved from -164% to -38%. This indicates **operating leverage is materializing** — revenue growth is far outpacing expense growth.

However, FY2025 free cash flow deteriorated sharply to -$322M (vs. -$116M in FY2024), primarily due to Neutron R&D capex increasing from $67M to $156M. This is a typical characteristic of an "investment phase."

### Duan Yongping-Style Interrogation

**What is good about this business? If you had to describe it in one sentence, what would it be?**

> The good: "space infrastructure construction" is a highly certain macro trend for the next 10-20 years, and Rocket Lab is the only company besides SpaceX that can provide a full-stack "from components to satellites to launches" service. The core advantage is **vertical integration creating cross-selling capability and customer lock-in** — customers who use your reaction wheels will likely buy your solar cells, and ultimately launch on your rockets.

**But this business also has clear weaknesses**:
- Not subscription-based; no certainty of recurring revenue
- Capital-intensive; requires continuous large investment
- Faces SpaceX, arguably the most formidable competitor in history
- Still unprofitable; depends on capital markets for funding

## Step 3: Economic Moat Assessment — Buffett's "Economic Moat"

### Five Moat Types Evaluated One by One

| Moat Type | Assessment | Strength | Evidence |
|-----------|------|------|------|
| **Brand / Pricing Power** | Moderate | ★★★☆☆ | Electron launch price ~$7.5M, below SpaceX rideshare but above SpaceX's marginal cost. Pricing power is limited; SpaceX's pricing is the industry ceiling |
| **Switching Costs** | Strong | ★★★★☆ | Satellite components (reaction wheels, star trackers, etc.) once designed-in are extremely costly to switch (requires re-certification, taking 6-18 months). Launch service switching costs themselves are low |
| **Network Effects** | Weak | ★★☆☆☆ | No traditional network effects exist. The "ecosystem effect" from vertical integration is not the same as network effects |
| **Scale Economies** | Moderate | ★★★☆☆ | Electron factory annual capacity 52 units; 21 launches in 2025, ~40% utilization. Scale growth will spread costs, but the gap vs. SpaceX is enormous |
| **Technology / IP Moat** | Strong | ★★★★☆ | Rutherford all-3D-printed electric pump engine, carbon composite rocket body, SolAero high-efficiency space solar cells (powering 1,000+ spacecraft). Technologically leading but not unreplicable |

### Moat Trend Judgment

**Past 5 years: Clearly widening**
- Evolved from a pure rocket company to a full-stack space company, establishing vertical integration through 7 acquisitions
- Backlog grew from zero to $2.2B
- Government contract credentials: from zero to NSSL Phase 3 Lane 1 selection ($5.6B total program)
- Became primary contractor for SDA missile defense satellites

**Next 5 years: Depends on Neutron**
- If Neutron succeeds: moat widens significantly — becomes one of only two reusable orbital launch providers globally (the other being SpaceX)
- If Neutron fails/significantly delayed: moat erodes — Electron faces squeeze from SpaceX rideshare and new entrants like Firefly

### One Underappreciated "Moat": ITAR Regime

The U.S. International Traffic in Arms Regulations (ITAR) effectively prohibit sensitive space payloads from launching on Chinese/Russian rockets. This creates a **structurally protected market** for U.S. launch providers like Rocket Lab. Western defense and intelligence payloads can only select U.S. or a small number of allied launch providers — a barrier that Chinese commercial rocket companies cannot overcome.

### Buffett-Style Interrogation

**Will this moat still exist in 10 years? What could destroy it?**

> The Space Systems (satellite component) moat is most durable — high switching costs, long certification cycles, and Rocket Lab has become an industry-standard supplier for multiple critical components. The launch business moat is more fragile — if SpaceX's Starship achieves ultra-low-cost launches, it could squeeze the entire small-to-medium rocket market to the margins.
>
> Forces that could destroy the moat: (1) SpaceX dramatically cutting prices, making dedicated small-sat launches economically unjustifiable; (2) Neutron failure, trapping Rocket Lab in the small rocket niche; (3) Chinese commercial rockets breaking through ITAR restrictions and capturing share in non-U.S. markets.

### Cross-Disciplinary Analysis

- **Technology adoption curve**: Space industry is on the steep ascent of the S-curve (similar to 1998-2002 internet), in the infrastructure build-out phase. Risk: in the 2000 dot-com bubble, many companies pointed in the "right direction" caused investors to lose money due to excessive valuations
- **Competitive game theory**: SpaceX is an "asymmetric competitor" — it has Starlink providing an internal demand loop; it doesn't need external customers to maintain high launch cadence. Rocket Lab entirely depends on external customers and is at a disadvantage in pricing dynamics
- **Survivorship bias**: We see Rocket Lab surviving and thriving, but in the same period Virgin Orbit went bankrupt, Astra nearly delisted, ABL pivoted away from rockets. Rocket Lab's success does not mean "space startups" are a good business — it shows precisely that this is an industry with an extremely high attrition rate

### Bias Self-Check

- **Narrative bias**: The "stars and oceans" space narrative is extremely compelling; easy for analysts to overestimate industry prospects and company success probability
- **Anchoring effect**: SpaceX's $1.77T IPO valuation could become an anchor, making RKLB's $55B valuation "seem cheap" — but the two companies differ enormously in scale and maturity
- **SpaceX proxy effect**: How much of RKLB's valuation premium is the "can't buy SpaceX so buy RKLB" substitution effect? After SpaceX IPO, this portion of the premium may rapidly dissipate

### Munger-Style Interrogation

**Where am I most likely to be wrong? Why would smart people not buy / short this company?**

> The most likely error: **being anchored to industry growth prospects and ignoring the absurdity of the valuation**. P/S 91x means that even if Rocket Lab triples revenue to $1.8B in the next 3 years, P/S would still be 30x — still expensive. Smart short sellers would say: (1) Neutron's success is far from certain; (2) after SpaceX IPO, RKLB's "proxy premium" will disappear; (3) the $3B ATM dilution plan signals that management itself believes the stock is overvalued. Bleecker Street Research is already short, with the core thesis that Neutron timelines are being whitewashed and the only disclosed contract comes from an unproven startup.

### Company's Position in Industry Value Chain

Rocket Lab occupies the **midstream** position of the space industry chain:
- Upstream: Raw materials, chips, basic components (not involved)
- **Midstream: Satellite component manufacturing + satellite integration + launch services (Rocket Lab's position)**
- Downstream: Satellite operations, data services, communications services (Starlink, etc.)

The advantage of this position is **it is on the necessary path of industry growth** — no matter who wins downstream, the midstream "water seller" benefits. The disadvantage is that margins are lower than downstream platform businesses (like Starlink), and it faces the complex relationship of being both a supplier to and a competitor of certain parties.

### Li Lu-Style Interrogation

**Looking back from 20 years from now, is this company "Standard Oil of this era" or "a flash-in-the-pan 3Com"?**

> The most likely outcome is neither Standard Oil nor 3Com, but **"Airbus of the space industry"** — under the dominance of SpaceX (Boeing analogy), becoming the world's second-largest space infrastructure company, with 15-25% market share, entrenched in defense and specific commercial sectors. That outcome is already quite good, but the question is: **does the current $55B valuation already fully, or even excessively, price in this "number two" value?**
